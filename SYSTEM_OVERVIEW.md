# Katiba OS — System Overview

*Working title in project docs: HakiFlow. Product name in the codebase and UI: Katiba OS.*

## What it is

Katiba OS is a legal workflow platform built for East Africa, starting with Kenya. It turns scattered evidence — voice notes, M-Pesa records, invoices, WhatsApp chats, receipts — into an organized, evidence-linked case that a human legal professional reviews and approves before anything is filed.

It is positioned as **one product with a shared trust layer**, not a collection of one-off AI chatbots. The same session-signing, role-based access, audit logging, and human-approval gate apply whether a citizen is preparing a small-claims case or a business is reviewing a supplier contract.

**Flagship use case:** a small-business owner is owed money, has four pieces of scattered evidence, and no legal background. Katiba OS turns that into a coherent, source-linked case file and a downloadable preparation pack in about ten minutes.

## The problem it addresses

The gap isn't a lack of legal information — it's a broken path from *"I have evidence and a grievance"* to *"I have an organized, review-ready case."* A citizen has facts spread across messages, receipts, and memory. A legal professional has to manually reconstruct those facts, spot the gaps, verify sources, and prepare documents. A generic chatbot answers questions but doesn't preserve evidence provenance or create a reviewable workflow — Katiba OS does both.

## Who uses it — three roles, one platform

| Role | What they do |
|---|---|
| **Citizen** (claimant) | Tells their story in plain language (English or Kiswahili, typed or spoken), uploads evidence, gets an AI-organized case with a plain-language summary and a clear next step |
| **Paralegal** | Works a review queue of citizen-submitted cases, verifies AI-flagged gaps, assigns cases, tracks readiness |
| **Lawyer** | Reviews AI findings against cited legal sources, approves or rejects the AI's output, signs off before anything moves forward |

Every AI-organized case carries a visible disclaimer and requires a named human's approval — the AI never closes a case, predicts an outcome, or files anything automatically.

## Key features

### Justice Engine (the flagship, fully working end-to-end)
- Guided 4-step intake: tell your story → claim details → add evidence → consent and review
- English and Kiswahili support, including real microphone transcription
- Evidence categorization (payment, agreement, communication, delivery, identity) with integrity checksums
- AI-built, evidence-linked timeline with a confidence score per event
- Explainable findings: strengths, gaps ("missing detail"), and next actions — every claim traces back to a source
- Legal citations are **allow-listed by the product, never generated by the model** — currently linked to the Kenya Law Small Claims Court Act and official Judiciary forms
- Basic Small Claims Court eligibility check (claim under KES 1,000,000, claim type in scope)
- Spoken AI case summaries, visibly disclosed as synthetic voice
- Branded, downloadable PDF preparation pack (claim summary, demand letter, evidence index, filing checklist)

### Contract Engine (demo-stage)
- Clause extraction and risk scoring against a Kenyan SME playbook
- Flags issues like unlimited indemnity, one-sided termination, and data-processing gaps, each with a suggested review position
- Contract health score and upcoming-obligations tracker

### Review & approval workflow
- Paralegal/lawyer review queue with status filtering and case assignment
- Human sign-off required before a case is marked approved — the AI cannot self-approve
- Full audit trail per case (who did what, when), exportable

### Trust, safety, and AI guardrails
- AI is constrained to organizing *supplied* facts and evidence metadata — instructed never to predict case outcomes, invent dates, or fabricate what a document proves
- Deterministic, non-AI fallback keeps the whole workflow functional even with no AI provider configured
- Schema-validated AI output (structured JSON, not free text) with a fixed shape the app controls
- Signed, expiring sessions; role-based access enforced on every API route (verified by attempting cross-role access, not just reading the code)
- Normalized relational data model (not a single JSON blob per case) — entities, evidence, timeline, issues, citations, and audit log are distinct tables

### Platform & operations views
- Analytics dashboard (cases by status, evidence volume, readiness trends)
- Security/trust center summarizing the platform's data-protection posture
- Cross-engine "Platform" view showing Justice, Contract, Compliance, and Evidence engines on the shared trust layer

### Delivery
- Installable Progressive Web App: offline-capable shell, responsive layout, dark mode, keyboard-navigable, reduced-motion aware
- A separate Flutter client (`apps/katiba_flutter`) targets Android, iOS, web, Windows, macOS, and Linux from one codebase

## Technology

**Web:** React 19, TypeScript, Vite, Express 5, Zod validation, Node's built-in SQLite, Recharts, a service-worker PWA.
**Mobile/desktop:** Flutter 3.44 / Dart, one codebase for five additional platforms.
**AI:** OpenAI Responses API for structured analysis and voice, with a deterministic safe-mode fallback when no key is configured — the app never breaks or fabricates data because AI is unavailable.
**Testing:** Vitest (API/security tests), Playwright (end-to-end + automated accessibility auditing via axe-core), Oxlint.

## Business model direction

Free or assisted access to the Justice Engine through legal-aid and public-interest partners; per-seat or per-document pricing for Contract/Compliance workspaces sold to SMEs and law firms; organization plans for larger legal teams. The intended sequence is to prove the small-claims workflow first, then extend the same trust layer to the other engines — not launch four disconnected products simultaneously.
